Reliable grounded VideoQA depends not only on predicting the correct answer, but also on identifying the temporal evidence that truly supports it. This requirement has become increasingly important as video understanding moves beyond answer-only evaluation toward grounded and faithful reasoning. \emph{NExT-GQA} shows that answer correctness does not necessarily imply correct evidence alignment \cite{xiao2024can}, while \emph{ReXTime} further highlights the difficulty of reasoning across time, where answer-supporting evidence may be temporally displaced from the question context \cite{chen2024rextime}.

Recent Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have substantially improved video understanding, including temporal grounding and long-video reasoning \cite{tang2025video, wu2025survey_vtg_mllm}. Temporal-aware grounding models such as \emph{VTimeLLM}, \emph{TimeChat}, and \emph{Grounded-VideoLLM} improve temporal sensitivity and localization ability \cite{huang2024vtimellm, ren2024timechat, wang2025grounded_videollm}. Modular reasoning frameworks such as \emph{SeViLA}, \emph{LLoVi}, and \emph{VideoMind} further show that decomposing video reasoning into specialized stages can improve interpretability and performance \cite{yu2023sevila, zhang2024simple, liu2025videomind}. However, existing methods still largely assume that the language used for answer prediction can be directly used for temporal evidence retrieval.

This assumption is problematic in grounded multiple-choice VideoQA. Answer candidates are written for discrimination, not for retrieval, and are therefore often abstract, incomplete, or weakly aligned with observable events. Temporal grounding, by contrast, requires queries that expose visually localizable evidence. When raw answer candidates are used as retrieval queries, the retrieved segments can be temporally nearby yet semantically weak, noisy, or only partially aligned with the evidence required for answering. This mismatch between answer formulation and temporal retrieval limits the reliability of grounded prediction.

After query refinement, the retriever receives a more visually localized description, which leads to better evidence alignment.

This paper proposes \emph{EviR}, an evidence-guided framework for temporally grounded VideoQA. EviR separates grounded reasoning into four stages: query reformulation, temporal retrieval, evidence validation, and answer generation. In the present multiple-choice setting, a Refiner rewrites each answer candidate into a retrieval-friendly query, a Retriever localizes candidate temporal spans, a Validator scores and ranks the retrieved evidence, and a Generator predicts the final answer from the highest-ranked clip. The central hypothesis is that grounded VideoQA becomes more reliable when answer semantics are decoupled from retrieval queries and when evidence quality is explicitly validated before answer generation.

Experiments on \emph{NExT-GQA} and \emph{ReXTime} support this hypothesis. EviR improves IoU@0.5 by 5.77 over the strongest prior baseline on \emph{NExT-GQA} and improves Acc@IoU=0.5 by 7.05 on \emph{ReXTime}, while also achieving the best or comparable answer accuracy. These results indicate that improving evidence quality leads directly to stronger grounded reasoning under strict grounding-aware evaluation.

The main contributions are summarized below.
\begin{itemize}
    \item A key mismatch in grounded VideoQA is identified between answer formulation and temporal evidence retrieval. On current multiple-choice benchmarks, answer candidates are useful for discrimination but often unsuitable for evidence localization.
    \item \emph{EviR} is presented as an evidence-guided framework that resolves this mismatch through query reformulation, temporal retrieval, evidence validation, and grounded answer generation.
    \item A role-specific optimization strategy is adopted to align each trainable module with its designated function, enabling more reliable evidence retrieval and evidence validation before answer generation.
    \item Strong results are achieved on \emph{NExT-GQA} and \emph{ReXTime}, showing that improving evidence quality leads to more accurate and more reliable grounded VideoQA under strict grounding-aware evaluation.
\end{itemize}
