
Understanding videos requires not only recognizing visual content but also identifying when relevant events occur. This requirement is especially critical in temporally grounded Video Question Answering (VideoQA), where a model must both predict the correct answer and localize the temporal evidence that justifies it. In this setting, answer correctness alone is insufficient: a prediction is reliable only if it is supported by faithful evidence rather than language priors or spurious visual correlations \cite{xiao2024can, chen2024rextime}.

Recent multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have made substantial progress in video understanding, including temporal grounding and long-video reasoning \cite{tang2025video, wu2025survey_vtg_mllm}. Methods such as VTimeLLM, TimeChat, and Grounded-VideoLLM improve temporal sensitivity and grounding ability, while recent reasoning frameworks such as SeViLA, LLoVi, and VideoMind demonstrate that decomposing video reasoning into multiple stages can enhance performance and interpretability \cite{huang2024vtimellm, ren2024timechat, wang2025grounded_videollm, yu2023sevila, zhang2024simple, liu2025videomind}. Despite these advances, a fundamental challenge remains underexplored in \emph{multiple-choice grounded VideoQA}.

Specifically, we observe a structural mismatch between \emph{answer formulation} and \emph{temporal evidence retrieval}. In multiple-choice VideoQA, answer candidates are designed for discrimination, often being abstract, incomplete, or weakly visual. However, temporal grounding requires queries that explicitly describe observable events. As a result, directly using answer candidates as retrieval queries leads to systematic errors: even strong temporal grounding models may retrieve segments that are temporally close but semantically weak or only partially aligned with the required evidence. This mismatch limits the effectiveness of existing approaches, which typically assume that answer representations can be directly grounded.

To address this problem, we propose a modular framework for temporally grounded VideoQA that explicitly separates \emph{evidence reformulation}, \emph{evidence acquisition}, \emph{evidence validation}, and \emph{answer generation}. Our framework consists of four components: a Refiner, a Retriever, a Validator, and a Generator. The Refiner first rewrites each answer candidate into a grounding-friendly query that exposes the underlying visual evidence. The Retriever then localizes candidate temporal spans for each refined query. Next, the Validator scores and ranks all retrieved candidates according to their reliability as supporting evidence. Finally, the Generator predicts the answer conditioned on the highest-ranked evidence rather than the entire video. This design enables each component to specialize in a distinct role, improving both evidence quality and grounded reasoning.

We evaluate our approach on NExT-GQA and ReXTime, two benchmarks that emphasize grounded answering and reasoning across time \cite{xiao2024can, chen2024rextime}. Experimental results show that our method consistently improves performance on strict grounding-aware metrics, indicating more precise and reliable evidence localization. Moreover, we observe corresponding gains in grounded answer accuracy, demonstrating that better evidence directly translates into more reliable predictions.

Our contributions are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We identify a key mismatch in multiple-choice grounded VideoQA: answer candidates are suitable for discrimination but not for temporal evidence retrieval, leading to systematic retrieval errors.
    \item We propose a modular framework that resolves this mismatch through query refinement and explicit evidence verification, improving the alignment between retrieval and reasoning.
    \item We achieve strong performance on NExT-GQA and ReXTime, demonstrating that improving evidence quality leads to more accurate and reliable grounded VideoQA.
\end{itemize}
